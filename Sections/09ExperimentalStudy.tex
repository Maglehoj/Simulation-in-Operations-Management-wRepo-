\section{Experimental Study}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Scenario tree}
\label{sec:scentree}
We use a two-stage design: one-factor-at-a-time (OFAT) screening to rank the
levers, followed by a $2^k$ factorial on the highest-impact levers, then a
demand sweep for 2030 and the Industry~4.0 scrap scenario. The OFAT branch is
ordered to test the analytically predicted constraints
(Sec.~\ref{sec:capacity}) first.

\subsection{Factorial design}
\label{sec:factorial}
OFAT screening ranks the three capacity stations above resequencing, so the
$2^3$ factorial is run on drying, cleaning-team and packaging capacity. A full
factorial (all eight corners, 20 replications each) is used rather than OFAT so
that interaction effects are estimable: OFAT would miss them and can misread
random variation between two runs as an effect.

\label{tab:factorial}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{l X X}
\toprule
Factor & Low ($-$) & High ($+$) \\
\midrule
A: Drying capacity        & 1 dryer  & 2 dryers \\
B: Cleaning-team capacity & 1        & 2 \\
C: Packaging capacity     & 3 lines  & 4 lines \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

Each effect is the difference between the four high-corner means and the four
low-corner means; its standard error is pooled from the within-corner variance
across all eight corners ($\mathrm{SE}=347$ bottles, $\mathrm{df}=152$), so each
effect carries a significance test.

Table~\ref{tab:doe} and the Pareto in Figure~\ref{fig:doe} show that \emph{no
effect is significant}: neither the three main effects (dryer, cleaning team,
packaging) nor any interaction can be distinguished from zero, the largest being
a $+454$-bottle packaging effect at $t=1.31$. At the fixed 50-batch plan no
capacity lever and no combination changes \emph{shipped output}, a result the
single-lever OFAT screening would have hidden, because output is fixed by
batches $\times$ per-batch yield, not by station capacity. Adding capacity moves
the bottleneck (Sec.~\ref{sec:btshift}) but not the throughput. This conclusion
is specific to the current order book: Section~\ref{sec:demand40} shows that once
the batch count itself grows toward 2030, capacity changes do shift lead time and
on-time delivery, though far less than the investment would suggest.

\subsection{Results: throughput across scenarios}
\label{sec:expresults}
Figure~\ref{fig:expresults} compares weekly throughput across the scenario set
with \SI{95}{\percent} confidence intervals. Almost every bar overlaps the
baseline: the capacity and sequencing levers do not move shipped output.
The one clear exception is Industry~4.0 (press scrap $10\rightarrow\SI{4}{\percent}$),
which lifts throughput by 4{,}842 bottles ($+\SI{8.0}{\percent}$,
95\% CI $[3{,}543,\,6{,}141]$), the only intervention whose interval
clears the baseline by a wide margin.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.72\textwidth]{fig_throughput.pdf}
\caption{Weekly throughput by scenario with \SI{95}{\percent} CI error bars.
Industry~4.0 (green) is the only scenario that separates from the baseline.}
\label{fig:expresults}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Queue discipline.} Switching the dryer queue from FIFO to EDD or SPT
(\texttt{queueStrategy}~$=1,2$) leaves throughput within the plan-capped scatter
(EDD $+\SI{2.6}{\percent}$, SPT $+\SI{1.5}{\percent}$), neither separated from the
baseline. Drying times are near-homogeneous ($\sigma\approx\SI{0.39}{\hour}$,
Sec.~\ref{sec:input}), so reordering a queue of similar jobs cannot meaningfully
change flow, and sequencing is not a lever here.

\subsection{Bottleneck shift across scenarios}
\label{sec:btshift}
We report per-station utilisation, not only throughput, to show \emph{which}
resource binds as capacity is added. Table~\ref{tab:btshift} and
Figure~\ref{fig:util} trace the bottleneck as it moves.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Mean utilisation (\%) by station. Bold $=$ binding constraint. The
bottleneck moves from drying to the cleaning team, while throughput stays flat.}
\label{tab:btshift}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{X c c c c}
\toprule
Scenario & Drying & Clean team & Packaging & Throughput \\
\midrule
Baseline              & \textbf{94.3} & 79.6 & 52.4 & 60{,}494 \\
$+1$ dryer            & 78.5 & \textbf{95.0} & 72.2 & 60{,}825 \\
$+1$ dryer, $+$clean team & 61.1 & \textbf{93.0} & 93.0 & 60{,}894 \\
Best combination ($+$dryer, $+$clean, $+$pack) & 78.2 & \textbf{92.8} & 76.7 & 60{,}908 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.72\textwidth]{fig_utilisation.pdf}
\caption{Resource utilisation by scenario. Adding a dryer pushes drying off the
\SI{90}{\percent} line but drives the cleaning team onto it, so the constraint
shifts rather than clears.}
\label{fig:util}
\end{figure}

Drying binds at baseline (\SI{94.3}{\percent}). A second dryer relieves it
(\SI{78.5}{\percent}) but immediately loads the cleaning team to
\SI{95.0}{\percent}, because each dryer carries a 2-hour clean that draws on the
same shared team. Adding a cleaning-team member then splits the load between the
team and packaging, both near \SI{93}{\percent}. Throughput never follows: it
holds at $\approx$60{,}500--61{,}000 bottles across all four configurations. This
is the bottleneck-shifting pattern: capacity relocates the constraint but cannot
raise output, because output is plan- and yield-limited, not capacity-limited.

\subsection{2030 demand and Industry 4.0}
\label{sec:demand40}
The 2030 question is one of \emph{volume}: management expects demand to keep
rising. We scale the order book through a \texttt{targetBatchCount} parameter that
regenerates the plan at 50, 75 and 100 batches, holding the product mix
(17/18/15) and the ten-batches-per-day cadence fixed, so only the volume changes.
(A secondary \texttt{demandMultiplier} lens, which merely compresses the release
of a fixed 50-batch plan, tests schedule resilience rather than the volume growth
asked here.) Table~\ref{tab:demand} reports the baseline configuration at each
volume.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Baseline configuration under genuine 2030 volume growth. Output scales
with the order book, but lead time and on-time delivery degrade sharply as the
single dryer saturates.}
\label{tab:demand}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{X c c c c c}
\toprule
Volume & Bottles & Dryer util. & Mean flow (h) & Avg WIP & On-time \\
\midrule
$N=50$ (today)  & 60{,}494  & \SI{94.3}{\percent} & 179 & 19.4 & \SI{100}{\percent} \\
$N=75$          & 92{,}649  & \SI{96.2}{\percent} & 260 & 28.3 & \SI{71}{\percent} \\
$N=100$         & 123{,}496 & \SI{96.8}{\percent} & 338 & 37.0 & \SI{54}{\percent} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

The line does \emph{produce} the extra volume (60{,}494 to 123{,}496 bottles),
but it does not absorb it gracefully. As the order book grows the single dryer
saturates (\SI{94.3}{\percent} to \SI{96.8}{\percent}), and the queue in front of
it drives \textbf{mean flow time from 179 to 338 h}, average WIP from 19 to 37,
and \textbf{on-time delivery from \SI{100}{\percent} to \SI{54}{\percent}}
(worst-case tardiness reaches 296 h at $N=100$). At current capacity the line
meets higher 2030 demand only by letting lead times roughly double and service
collapse (Figure~\ref{fig:volume}).

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.62\textwidth]{fig_volume.pdf}
\caption{Service under 2030 volume growth: mean flow time (bars, \SI{95}{\percent}
CI) and on-time delivery (line) versus order-book size. Output scales with $N$,
but lead time roughly doubles and on-time delivery falls from \SI{100}{\percent}
to \SI{54}{\percent}.}
\label{fig:volume}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Capacity combinations under volume growth.}
Adding capacity at volume shifts the constraint but barely recovers service. At
$N=100$ a second dryer relieves drying (\SI{96.8}{\percent} to \SI{85.3}{\percent})
yet immediately saturates the shared cleaning team (\SI{97.5}{\percent}), because
each dryer carries a 2-hour clean drawn from the same team; mean flow improves
only 338 to 330 h and on-time delivery 54 to \SI{55}{\percent}. Adding a
cleaning-team member as well leaves the team still at \SI{96.4}{\percent} and flow
at 327 h. The dryer-clean coupling on the shared team is therefore a
\emph{structural} constraint that incremental capacity relocates but does not
resolve. Restoring service at 2030 volume requires decoupling the dryer clean from
the shared team (a dedicated or faster clean), not simply buying one more machine.

\paragraph{Industry 4.0: predictive maintenance at the press.}
The scenario models condition-based (predictive) maintenance: real-time sensors
monitor press force and filling depth and flag the drift before the press leaves
specification, so a developing fault is corrected before it scraps tablets rather
than after. The QA estimate is that this cuts press scrap from \SI{10}{\percent}
to \SI{4}{\percent}. Because output is bounded by batches $\times$ yield, raising
good tablets per batch is the one intervention that lifts \emph{shipped}
throughput: 65{,}336 vs.\ 60{,}494 bottles, a gain of \textbf{4{,}842 bottles per
run} ($+\SI{8.0}{\percent}$, 95\% CI $[3{,}543,\,6{,}141]$). It is the only lever
that significantly raises shipped output, where no capacity lever does, and it
needs only the sensor retrofit, not added line capacity. The same yield gain
carries to volume: applied together with a second dryer at $N=100$
(\texttt{dryer2\_batches100}), the scrap reduction lifts good bottles from
\num{122448} to \num{130345}, a further \SI{6.4}{\percent}.

\subsection{Managerial value: benefit vs.\ effort}
\label{sec:value}
\begin{table}[H]

\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{X c X}
\toprule
Lever & $\Delta$ throughput & Implementation effort / cost \\
\midrule
Resequence (group by product) & $-0.8\%$ (n.s.) & Free: scheduling change only \\

$+1$ dryer                    & $-0.3\%$ (n.s.) & Medium: equipment, moderate lead time \\
$+1$ packaging line           & $+0.8\%$ (n.s.) & High: capex, long lead time (the 2030 plan defers this) \\

\end{table}

Only one lever moves throughput at the current order book: the press-sensor
retrofit ($+\SI{8.0}{\percent}$), at a fraction of the capital cost of any line
investment. The capacity levers (more dryers, cleaning staff, packaging,
resequencing) return nothing measurable on output at $N=50$; their role appears
only under volume growth (Sec.~\ref{sec:demand40}), where they shift the
constraint and modestly ease lead time but do not, on their own, restore service.
