\section{Conclusions and Insights}
\label{sec:conclusions}

% ~0.75 page. Managerial takeaways, tied to the KPIs.
The baseline identifies drying as the primary constraint, at \SI{94.9}{\percent}
utilisation and with the highest required unit-hours. The cleaning team is second
at \SI{79.6}{\percent}, and packaging, although it has the largest absolute
workload, is only third at \SI{52.2}{\percent} because it runs on three parallel
lines. No capacity lever raises throughput. A second dryer relieves drying but
moves the bottleneck onto the cleaning team (\SI{79.6}{\percent} to
\SI{94.8}{\percent}), since each dryer adds a two-hour clean, and adding cleaning
staff then pushes the constraint onto packaging. Shipped output stays at
60{,}000--62{,}000 bottles in every case, because the volume is fixed by the
50-batch plan and the per-batch yield, not by station capacity. The $2^3$
factorial confirms this: of the seven effects only a fourth packaging line is
significant, and only by $+717$ bottles ($+\SI{1.2}{\percent}$). Resequencing by
product changes throughput by $-\SI{0.6}{\percent}$, which is not significant,
and its only benefit is a small reduction in flow time.

Towards 2030 the line absorbs demand compressed up to $\times2.0$ with zero
release violations. Congestion grows, with flow time up \SI{13}{\percent} and WIP
up \SI{14}{\percent}, but no unbounded backlog forms, and drying, not the
schedule, is the resource that would bind first if the batch volume itself grew.
The decisive lever is Industry~4.0. Cutting press scrap from \SI{10}{\percent} to
\SI{4}{\percent} raises shipped throughput by 4{,}764 bottles per run
($+\SI{7.8}{\percent}$, 95\% CI $[3{,}479,\,6{,}050]$), which is more than six
times the only significant capacity effect, and it needs only a one-off sensor
retrofit with no added line capacity and no recurring cost. Our recommendation to
management is therefore to defer the capital expansion that the 2030 plan was
built around, and to invest instead in scrap reduction at the tablet press.
